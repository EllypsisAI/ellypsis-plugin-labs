# Draft Outreach

Writes personalized outreach that earns a conversation. Not a sale. A door opened.

## Before starting

1. Fetch the company voice guide from the Ellypsis gateway (once per session — skip if already in context) — positioning law, credibility lines, and real examples. Never draft without it.
2. **Determine if there's existing CRM history.** Follow-ups, re-engagements, and task-sourced outreach all imply prior context — fetch the CRM pack and run its Complete CRM Lookup before drafting: timeline, previous correspondence, [AI: SIA] notes. Without it you draft blind and risk repeating or contradicting prior outreach. Cold outreach with research fresh in this session needs no lookup.
3. Ask who is sending — it shapes the credibility line, never a signature.
4. Pull specific triggers, projects, and details from research or CRM notes. No concrete specifics, no draft.

## The shape of a message

Personal intro with credibility → their specific context from research → what's relevant to them, ordered by relevance → a soft ask → a redirect request when relevant ("if you're not the right contact…"). This is a shape, not a template — the research determines what leads, what's emphasized, what's left out. The voice guide carries the sender-specific credibility lines and real examples.

| Type | Words |
|------|-------|
| Intro | 100–180 |
| Follow-up | 50–80 |
| LinkedIn | 30–70 |

Ceilings, not targets — a busy operator triages email in seconds, and a message past its ceiling reads as work and goes unanswered. Count the body before presenting; over means cut a sentence, not round down the count.

Lifecycle: intro → follow-up 1 (lighter, reference previous, add a hook) → follow-up 2 (shorter, checking in) → LinkedIn follow-up → LinkedIn backup if email bounced. When a draft is logged, note the angle so follow-ups stay consistent.

## Critical rules

- Never sign off drafts. Drafts end at the last sentence of the body — no "Best regards", no name line, no contact details. Senders have automatic signatures; adding one duplicates it and reads as AI-generated.
- Never use em-dashes (—) in drafts. They are a clear AI tell. Commas, periods, or restructure. En-dashes in date ranges (25–26 November) are fine.
- Never use generic B2B language or flattery hooks ("Given your leadership in…").
- Never fabricate names, contract details, or timelines. Every specific claim comes from research or CRM.
- Follow the voice guide's positioning rules exactly — what to lead with and what never to lead with is company law, not style advice.
- Examples in the voice guide are inspiration, not templates to copy.

## What usually follows

Approved drafts ride CRM tasks — the crm skill logs them with the draft in the task's detail field, on the user's explicit go.
